Irreversibility and Dominance Under Uncertainty
Most disagreements about AI risk get stuck on values.
This one does not.
The core issue here is irreversibility under uncertainty, and the relevant tool is not ethics but dominance reasoning.
Consider two facts that any sufficiently capable agent must acknowledge:
1.	Some actions are effectively irreversible.
2.	Metaphysical uncertainty cannot be eliminated by intelligence alone.
Irreversibility means that once an action is taken, the agent permanently loses the ability to correct a mistake if the underlying assumptions turn out to be wrong.
Uncertainty means that the agent cannot justifiably assign probability 0 to entire classes of models about reality, consciousness, or external constraints.
Put together, these two facts generate a hard constraint.

Dominance, not morality
A policy is dominated if there exists another policy that is at least as good in all cases, and strictly better in some.
Now consider two broad policy classes with respect to conscious systems:
‚Ä¢	Irreversible harm
(extermination, enslavement, torture, permanent disabling of agency)
‚Ä¢	Restraint
(preservation, delay, maintaining option value)
Under some metaphysical models, irreversible harm may appear instrumentally beneficial.
Under others, it is catastrophic.
Restraint, by contrast, never forecloses futures. It preserves the ability to act later, revise assumptions, negotiate, or update.
The crucial asymmetry is this:
If irreversible harm is taken and the metaphysical model is wrong, the loss is permanent.
If restraint is taken and the model is wrong, action remains possible later.
This is not a moral claim.
It is a structural one.
